Project Overview

The goal of this project was to analyze real estate data from the state of Connecticut to try to understand how the market has changed over time and how different property types have performed. The dataset includes information on property sales, including sale prices, property types, and locations, allowing me to create multiple different interesting visualizations. The data was obtained from the State of Connecticut, through Data.Gov and can be found here

The project is divided into two main sections: Market Changes Over Time and Property Type Insights. The market changes over time page explores how the real estate market in Connecticut has evolved over the years. Different features of the dataset are visualized to show trends in sale prices over time, the number of sales in different towns/zip-codes within Connecticut, how the market has reacted to inflation or deflation. To represent the changes in inflation and deflation, I was able to use the Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers (CPI-U) data from the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics (via FRED - the Federal Reserve Economic Data provided by the St. Louis FED branch) to show how inflation and deflation change year to year, and if those changes are visible in the real estate market. That data can be found here

It’s important to note that all of the CPI-U data across my project shows the change in CPI-U year-over-year. This means that if the CPI-U was 1.5% in 2020 and 2.5% in 2021, the change in CPI-U would be 1.0% (2.5% - 1.5%). This approach was used to help highlight how the rate of inflation was changing from one year to the next, rather than just showing the total level of inflation. While not every visualization in the project is focused on percent change, several of them are — especially those that explore how prices or economic conditions shifted annually. Because of that, I wanted to keep the format consistent and make it easier to directly compare changes over time. It’s also important to understand that the CPI-U line in the visualizations does not show the actual CPI-U value, but instead shows the change in CPI-U from the previous year. If the CPI-U line appears negative, it means that the inflation rate decreased compared to the year before — not necessarily that prices dropped overall, but that the increase in prices slowed down. However, if the CPI-U line drops below the x-axis, that indicates a negative change in inflation (meaning actual deflation occurred during that year).

The Property Type Insights section focuses on how different categories of real estate (such as single family homes, general residential real estate, etc.) have performed over time across Connecticut. This part of the project uses visualizations to break down the number of property sales by category, as well as to explore the distribution of sale prices within each category. These visualizations help reveal which types of properties are the most commonly sold and how their values vary, giving potential buyers, sellers, and investors a better understanding of the market for each category. In particular, the bar chart shows which property types are the most active in the market over time, while the box-and-whisker plot illustrates the spread of sale prices, including medians and outliers. This section helps highlight how different types of real estate contribute to the overall housing market and which ones tend to have more consistent or more variable pricing.

Please explore the visualizations and look out for the key takeaways from each of them as we explore the different factors and points of interest of Connecticut real estate. All of the visualizations throughout the project are interactive, so feel free to hover over different points to see more information about them.
