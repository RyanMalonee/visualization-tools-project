Understanding Property Type Trends

This section explores how different categories of real estate have performed across Connecticut. From single-family homes to condos and commercial properties, we look at both how often they are sold and how their prices vary.

Once visualizations are added, you'll be able to interact with the data to see which property types are most active in the market, and how sale prices are distributed within each group.

Sales Volume by Property Type

This bar chart shows the number of sales for each property type. Users can filter by the dates of the sales to see how the number of sales has changed over time. This helps highlight which types of properties are most popular, consistently in demand, and which may have experienced growth or decline in popularity over the years.
*Please note that many home sale records did not have a record of the property type - those have been filtered out in an effort to only report accurate information.*
Looking at the visualization above, we can see that the most commonly sold property type, across all years available (4/5/1999 - 8/31/2023), is Single Family homes, with a total of 401,612 sales. This is followed quickly by other residential properties. Unsurprisingly, the second least common property type sold is industrial real estate, with only 774 sales recorded. This makes sense considering most individuals aren't in a position to purchase industrial-grade properties, which are typically owned by businesses. Their high median sale price, as seen in the next visualization, also contributes to their limited number of transactions. You'll also notice "Public Utility" is the least common property type sold, with only 10 sales recorded. This is likely due to the fact that public utilities are typically owned/managed by the government, or there are limited public utility properties available for sale across the state.

Sale Price Distribution by Property Type (Box Plot)

This box-and-whisker plot highlights the distribution of sale prices across the different property types. The box plots will show the median, quartiles, and outliers for each category, helping users understand the typical price ranges and variability in pricing for each type of real estate. Users will be able to filter by the dates of the sales to see how the median sale prices and distributions have changed over time. The max sales price filter can be adjusted to include higher or lower sales and exclude more or less outliers, allowing users to see how the distribution of sale prices changes when outliers are included or excluded (a higher maximum sales price will include more outliers).
Looking at the visualization above, we can see that the median sale price for Single Family homes is higher than that of Two and Three Family homes. This may not be surprising, as single-family homes often come with more land and privacy, which can drive up their value (more people want to purchase them). However, something interesting depicted by the box-and-whisker plots is the broad category of "Residential" property types has a much higher median price than Single Family, Two Family, and Three Family homes, suggesting that the "Residential" category may include high-value property types beyond standard homes.
