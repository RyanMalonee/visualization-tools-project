Exploring Market Changes Over Time

This section focuses on how real estate prices across Connecticut have changed year by year, and how those trends relate to other economic indicators like inflation or deflation. By looking at real estate data and Year-Over-Year changes in the Consumer Price Index (CPI-U), we can observe patterns in how quickly home prices have risen or slowed down over time, how these changes compare to inflation, and how different property types have changed in value over time.

These visualizations are designed to highlight major shifts in the market and help users better understand how sale prices and assessed values compare to inflation across different years.

Median Sale Price vs. Median Assessed Value Over Time

The visualization below depicts the median sale prices of Connecticut real estate over the years, the median assessed values of those properties, and the difference between them. As you look at the visualization, you’ll notice a bar between the two lines representing the difference between sale price and assessed value. A larger bar indicates a larger difference. Hover over the visualization to view tooltip information including the sale year, median assessed value ($), median sale value ($), and the difference between them ($).
After looking at the above visualization, we can see that the year with the largest difference between median sale price and median assessed value is 2023, with a gap of $162,460. This could be explained by a number of factors, including elevated home prices driven by limited housing supply in the aftermath of the COVID-19 pandemic. (See the External Resources page to learn more about the pandemic's impact.)

2011 marks the year with the lowest difference between median sale price and median assessed value, with a gap of only $30,846. This could be explained by the fact that the housing market was still recovering from the financial crisis, which caused a significant drop in home prices and assessed values. As a result, the median sale price and median assessed value were able to become more closely aligned during this time.

Year-Over-Year Change in Median Sale Prices

This line graph shows the Year-Over-Year change in median sale price for each property type from 2006 to 2023. It also includes a line (light grey) representing the Year-Over-Year change in the Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers (CPI-U), helping highlight periods of inflation and deflation. When the CPI-U line rises above zero, it indicates increasing inflation compared to the previous year at the same time; when it dips below, it signals deflation or slowed inflation. By comparing these trends side-by-side, users can observe how different property types responded to broader economic conditions, such as the 2008 financial crisis and the COVID-19 pandemic (highlighted in light red). Hover over the visualization to view tooltip information or alter the filters to get a better view of the data.

*Note, not all property types are known, records of unknown property types can be filtered out. All properties are displayed by default*
Looking at the visualization, we can see that during the 2008-2009 financial crisis, the family homes (four family, three family, two family, and single family homes) were some of the property types hit the hardest. Three family homes had a -42.9% change in median sale prices compared to the year prior. At this same period of time, CPI-U was down -0.338% from the year prior. This indicates that the effects of the financial crisis were felt in Connecticut when it occurred. When we filter the data to only show those "family homes", we can see that, during 2008-2009, three family homes were hit the hardest, followed by four family homes, and then two family homes. Single family homes were the least affected, but still saw a -13% change in 2008, and a -8.81% change in 2009.

Sale Prices Over Time with CPI-U

This scatter plot shows the total sale amounts recorded in Connecticut from 1999 to 2023. Each green point represents a recorded property sale, while the yellow line displays the Year-Over-Year % change in the Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers (CPI-U). The CPI-U line provides helpful context about periods of inflation and deflation, allowing users to compare how the economy may align with real estate activity over time. Users can interact with the filters on the right-hand side to explore the data in more detail. The “Sale Amount” filter can be used to remove outliers (such as extremely high-value sales) which may visually compress the rest of the graph. It’s also useful for viewing sales within a specific price range. The “Town” filter allows users to narrow the data by a specific location within Connecticut to observe trends in specific towns/areas.

*CPI-U data only goes back as far as August 2006, so anything before that is shown as a 0% change. Data may be slow at loading due to size.*
The visualization shows that the highest sale price recorded in Connecticut occurred in August 2021, with a sale price of $5,000,000,000 for apartments (serial number 20200091). In addition to this, we can see that from July 2015 on, the CPI-U Year-Over-Year % change has been above 0%, indicating inflation. The highest CPI-U Year-Over-Year % change occurred in June 2022, with a 9.06% increase. This is likely due to the economic recovery following the COVID-19 pandemic, when consumer demand surged, supply chains were still disrupted, and inflation rose sharply across the country. If we filter the data down to just family home sales (single family, two family, three family, and four family properties), we see that the highest home price was $120,000,000 in April 2014, but most of the prices were under $20,000,000. When we continue to filter the data further, and set a max sale price of $20,000,000, we can see that there are a lot of sales in the $0 - $8,000,000 range, with fewer sales reaching as high up as $20,000,000. This is to be expected, as the majority of Americans would not be able to afford a home in the $20,000,000 range.
